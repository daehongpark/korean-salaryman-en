Navigating Seoul's Metro: Essential Korean Subway Etiquette for Tourists

S
Korean Salaryman
June 25, 2026
Read time 16 min
SUMMARY

Mastering Korean subway etiquette for tourists ensures a smooth and respectful experience on Seoul's highly efficient metro system. By understanding local norms like priority seating, quiet conduct, and efficient boarding, visitors can navigate like a local and avoid common faux pas.

📅 Updated 2026.06.25

You're probably looking at your Seoul itinerary, wondering how to get around. The subway, or jihacheol (지하철), is the undisputed king of transport here. It's fast, clean, and connects everything. But unlike some places, there's an unspoken rulebook to navigating it, and knowing your Korean subway etiquette for tourists isn't just polite—it makes your whole trip smoother.

### Getting Your Ticket to Ride: T-Money and Apps

The most important thing to know is that you'll need a T-money card (a rechargeable transportation card) or a compatible mobile payment. Don't even think about fumbling for single-use tickets for every ride; it’s inefficient and slows everyone down. As of June 25, 2026, a T-money card costs around 3,000-5,000 won (roughly $2-4 USD) to purchase at any convenience store or subway station vending machine, then you load it up with cash. The basic fare for a single ride within Seoul is currently around 1,400 won (about $1 USD), with additional charges for longer distances. My coworker still uses a physical T-money card, but I’ve moved to just tapping my phone with a registered card; it’s much faster. Download Naver Map or Kakao Metro for real-time train schedules and navigation – these are non-negotiable.

Point: Get a T-money card or set up mobile pay first; it’s the gateway to everything.

### The Golden Rules of Seating: Priority and Etiquette

Always, and I mean always, yield priority seats. These aren't just suggestions; they are sacred spaces reserved for the elderly, pregnant women, people with disabilities, and sometimes, young children. You'll see them clearly marked, often in pink or yellow. Even if the train is packed, these seats will often remain empty. It’s a core part of our nunchi (the art of reading the room and acting appropriately) to leave them open. I’ve seen tourists plop down in an empty priority seat, oblivious, and get some serious side-eye. No one will say anything directly, but trust me, people are judging. If an elderly person boards, even if you’re not in a priority seat, it’s good form to offer yours if no priority seats are available. It’s ingrained in us from childhood.

Point: Priority seats are non-negotiable; don't sit there unless you qualify, and even then, be ready to offer your seat.

### Boarding, Alighting, and the Palli-Palli Flow

Korean subways operate with serious palli-palli (hurry-hurry, do-it-now mindset). When the train arrives, stand to the sides of the doors, letting passengers off first. Never try to push your way in before people have fully exited. There’s enough room; just wait your turn. When you get on, try to move deeper into the carriage instead of lingering by the door, especially during rush hour. I learned this the hard way during my first month commuting; I was practically blocking the door trying to find a spot for my backpack and got a gentle shove from an ajumma (middle-aged woman, often used affectionately or to describe a certain type of strong-willed woman). When you're getting off, try to be near the door a stop or two before your destination. If you're stuck deep inside, a polite, quiet "Naerigil-geyo" (내리길게요 – "I'm getting off") or "Jamsimanyo" (잠시만요 – "Excuse me") with a slight bow or hand gesture will usually get people to move.

Point: Let people off first, move into the car, and be ready to exit efficiently.

### The Unwritten Rules of the Ride: Noise, Food, and Space

Keep the noise down. This isn't a place for loud phone conversations, booming music, or boisterous chatter with your travel buddies. Most people are either silently commuting, scrolling on their phones, or trying to nap. Phone calls should be short and quiet, ideally stepped off the train if lengthy. Eating on the subway is generally frowned upon, especially anything with a strong smell like tteokbokki (spicy rice cakes) or fried chicken. Drinking coffee or water is usually fine, but save the full meals for outside. Personal space is a fluid concept during rush hour, but otherwise, try not to lean on others or spread out too much. My biggest pet peeve is when people put their backpacks on the empty seat next to them during off-peak hours when someone else could sit. Always put your bag on your lap or between your feet.

Point: Be quiet, don't eat smelly food, and respect personal space, even if it feels minimal sometimes.

### Escalator Etiquette and Station Navigation

When using escalators, stand on the right side if you're not in a hurry. The left side is for those who want to walk or run up/down. This isn't as strictly enforced everywhere as it used to be (some stations even encourage standing on both sides for safety), but it’s still generally good practice, especially in busy stations. Navigating transfers can be confusing. Look for the color-coded lines and directional signs, which are usually in English too. Don't stop abruptly in the middle of a busy walkway to check your map; step aside to avoid blocking the human traffic flow. I remember once, during a massive hoesik (the after-work company dinner), trying to guide my extremely tipsy boss through Gangnam Station and the sheer volume of people was dizzying. Staying aware of the flow is key. And remember, keep your voice down, even when asking for directions; a quiet, polite inquiry goes a long way.

Point: Keep the flow moving, use maps off to the side, and don’t be a bottleneck.

### What About Luggage and Late Nights?

Yes, you can bring luggage on the subway. Just be mindful of others, especially during peak hours. Try to keep large suitcases out of the main aisle and consolidate your bags. During my first international trip back to Korea, I awkwardly dragged two huge suitcases during rush hour, and it was a nightmare. Learn from my mistakes. Late at night, the subway is generally safe, but services typically stop around midnight or a little after. Check your app for last train times. While the atmosphere might be a bit quieter with fewer people, the same etiquette applies. Don't be too loud, avoid drawing unnecessary attention to yourself, and be aware of your surroundings, just as you would anywhere else. Koreans value public order, and that extends to late-night transit too.

Point: Luggage is fine with awareness; late-night subways are safe but respect the peace and check last train times.
