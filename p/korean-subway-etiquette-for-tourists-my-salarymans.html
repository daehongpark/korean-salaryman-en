Korean Subway Etiquette for Tourists: My Salaryman's Guide to Not Looking Lost

S
Korean Salaryman
July 2, 2026
Read time 20 min
SUMMARY

Mastering Korean subway etiquette means understanding the unspoken rules around escalators, priority seating, noise levels, and boarding procedures. Using a T-Money card and being mindful of local customs ensures a smooth and respectful travel experience in Seoul.

📅 Updated 2026.07.02

You're planning a trip to Seoul, and you've probably heard our subway system is incredible. It is — it's fast, efficient, and connects everything. But beyond T-Money cards and transfer lines, there's a whole world of unspoken rules that make it run so smoothly. As someone who rides it twice a day, every day, I can tell you that understanding these isn't just about being polite; it's about blending in and avoiding the subtle side-eye that even locals give each other when someone breaks the unwritten code. This guide on korean subway etiquette for tourists will help you navigate like a local.

The Unofficial Fast Lane: Stand Right, Walk Left (Mostly)

Always stand on the right side of escalators; the left is for walking, but sometimes even that's frowned upon if you're not actually moving. This is probably the number one rule I see foreigners (and sometimes even young Koreans) accidentally break. Korea runs on palli-palli (the hurry-hurry, do-it-now mindset), and nowhere is that more evident than on a crowded subway escalator during rush hour. People are literally running late for work, and blocking the left lane is a cardinal sin. I've been one of those people silently fuming behind a group of tourists blocking the entire escalator because they're admiring the station art. While technically the rule is "stand right, walk left," there's a growing sentiment, especially in newer or less crowded stations, that it's safer to just stand still on either side. But if you see people actively walking on the left, make sure you're not in their way.

Point: Locals hate escalator blockers more than almost anything else; keep the left clear for sprinters.

Prioritizing the Precious: Who Gets the Seats, and When

Certain seats are strictly reserved for the elderly, pregnant women, and disabled passengers, even if they appear empty. You'll see these seats clearly marked with specific colors or icons – often pink for pregnant women, and yellow or blue for the elderly and disabled. This isn't just a suggestion; it's a deep-seated cultural expectation rooted in respect (jeong, a deep, sticky sense of attachment and care for others). I once saw a young student, probably exhausted after hagwon (private cram school), get a stern, silent glare from an older woman simply for sitting in a priority seat on a relatively empty train. He immediately jumped up and moved. My own grandmother would have given me a talking-to if she ever saw me in one. These seats are meant to be available at all times for those who need them, not just when you see someone approaching. Seriously, just don't sit there.

Point: Don't even think about sitting in the priority seats, no matter how tired your legs are from sightseeing.

Noise Control: Keep Your K-Drama Binge to Yourself

Keep phone conversations and media playback volume low, or better yet, use headphones. Public spaces in Korea, especially on the subway, are generally quiet environments. People are often lost in their own worlds – reading webtoons, playing mobile games, or just staring blankly after a long day at the office. Loud phone conversations or blaring music from speakers are considered incredibly rude and disruptive. My commute is about 45 minutes, and I use that time to catch up on podcasts. My headphones are always in, and the volume is never high enough for the person next to me to hear. I even saw a coworker on the train once; his phone rang, and he just mumbled a quick "I'll call you back" before hanging up and sending a text. That's how ingrained the quiet expectation is here.

Point: Your playlist or phone call is not for everyone's enjoyment; personal space includes auditory space.

Boarding and Alighting: The Dance of the Doors

Always let passengers exit the train first before attempting to board. This might seem like common sense, but it's a rule that's strictly adhered to here and is a core part of korean subway etiquette for tourists. When a train pulls into the station, people naturally form two lines on either side of the doors, leaving the center clear for those getting off. It's an unspoken choreography that ensures smooth passenger flow. I've had to physically step aside more than once because a tourist, eager to get on, was blocking the exit. The train doors chime several times before closing, so there’s no need to rush and push your way in. Just wait patiently; there will be space, or another train will be along in a few minutes.

Point: Patience is key; don't be "that guy" blocking the flow, let people off first.

T-Money & Transfers: Your Digital Key to the City

Use a T-Money card for seamless travel and discounted transfers across buses and subways. Forget fumbling for cash or buying single-use tickets every time. The T-Money card is your best friend for navigating public transport in Seoul, and it’s a must-have for any visitor. You can buy one for about ₩2,500 (roughly $1.80 USD as of mid-2026) at any convenience store (like CU, GS25, 7-Eleven) or at vending machines in subway stations. You then top it up with cash. A single subway ride is typically around ₩1,250 (about $0.90 USD), but the T-Money card allows for free or heavily discounted transfers between subway lines and buses within a 30-minute window (or 60 minutes after 9 PM). I've had the same T-Money card since my university days; it’s practically glued to my wallet. I just top it up at the convenience store near my office when the balance gets low.

Point: The T-Money card isn't just convenient; it's the default way locals travel and saves you money.

Eating, Drinking, and Cleaning Up Your Act

Generally, no eating or drinking on the train, and always dispose of your trash properly. While it’s not strictly forbidden by official rules like in some other countries, eating strong-smelling food on the train is definitely frowned upon. People usually finish their coffees or snacks before boarding. You might see someone with a bottled drink, but they’ll keep it sealed. One thing to be aware of is the surprising lack of public trash cans in Korea, including on subway platforms and inside trains. This means you often have to carry your trash with you until you find a proper bin, usually outside the station or back at your accommodation. My company recently participated in a city-wide clean-up initiative, and it really highlighted how much effort goes into keeping public spaces clean, often by individuals carrying their own waste.

Point: Keep your snacks for the platform, or better yet, outside, and be prepared to hold onto your trash.

Respecting the Culture of Quiet and Efficiency

Ultimately, the best korean subway etiquette for tourists boils down to observing and respecting the general atmosphere of quiet efficiency. The Seoul subway system is a marvel because everyone generally understands and follows these unwritten rules, contributing to a smooth, peaceful, and incredibly fast commuting experience. Pay attention to what locals are doing, be mindful of your surroundings, and remember that you’re sharing a public space with millions of people going about their busy lives. Follow these tips, and you’ll blend in like a pro, making your travels in Seoul much more pleasant for yourself and everyone else.

Point: A little observation and consideration go a long way in navigating Seoul's subway like a true insider.
